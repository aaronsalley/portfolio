Intry was founded on a simple but uncomfortable truth:
modern hiring systems ask people to distort themselves in order to be seen.
As a cofounder, my role was to define how the product should feel before deciding how it should work. The challenge wasn’t designing screens—it was designing a system of representation that respected real experience while operating inside an automated hiring ecosystem.
The product didn’t need to look innovative.
It needed to be trustworthy.

Job seekers were navigating two invisible challenges at once.
First, they had to infer what skills and signals actually mattered beneath vague job descriptions. Then they had to encode that understanding into artifacts optimized for applicant tracking systems—often at the expense of voice, personality, and nuance.
Compounding the problem, many candidates weren’t even aware that their resume was being evaluated by automated systems—let alone that it needed to be legible to a machine before a human would ever see it.
The surface issue looked like formatting.
The deeper issue was misrepresentation by design.
Most hiring tools optimized output without questioning the model itself. They treated resumes and assessments as static artifacts, not as interfaces between people and systems.

Hiring systems operate at scale, but understanding does not.
When representation breaks down, systems reward optimization while humans receive distorted signal. Candidates contort experience to survive screening. Employers evaluate flattened profiles divorced from context, motivation, and judgment.
As a cofounder and UX designer, the risk wasn’t poor usability—it was institutionalizing misrepresentation through design.
The goal wasn’t efficiency or engagement.
It was preserving meaning as experience moved through automated systems.

The foundational UX decision was to treat representation as a continuum, not a document.
Instead of designing a resume builder, I designed a representation system—one where a candidate’s skills, experience, motivations, and traits existed as a stable source of truth, and outputs became contextual expressions of that truth rather than rewrites of it.
To operate credibly inside real hiring ecosystems, we integrated best-in-class capabilities—but deliberately kept them behind the interface.

- Sovren ensured resumes behaved predictably inside applicant tracking systems. From a UX perspective, this removed the need for candidates to understand parsing rules or formatting constraints—the system handled structural translation quietly and consistently.
- Traitify and the Big Five personality model informed how personality and motivation were captured. Rather than surfacing scores or labels, the UX translated these signals into narrative context—supporting human interpretation without reducing identity.
- Pairin influenced how skills and competencies were normalized and mapped to role requirements, allowing transferable skills and non-linear paths to be understood without exposing taxonomies or jargon.

Across the platform, automation was deliberately constrained. AI and third-party systems operated at the level of translation and framing, never authorship. Users could always see how their experience was being interpreted and emphasized—without ever feeling rewritten or overridden.
As a cofounder, this meant designing boundaries as much as flows—deciding what the product should never do, even if the technology allowed it.

The resulting experience allowed candidates to move through automated hiring systems without sacrificing authenticity.
Users spent less time rewriting resumes and more time applying with confidence. Profiles remained stable even as representations adapted to different contexts. Hiring managers encountered clearer signal earlier—contextualized, comparable, and human.
For Intry, the UX established a differentiated position in a crowded market—not as a resume builder or assessment tool, but as a platform for faithful professional representation.

Design doesn’t become ethical by being transparent.
It becomes ethical by preserving meaning under transformation.
